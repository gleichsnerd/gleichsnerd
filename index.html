Some things I've worked on

Paxata

It's dangerous to go alone, take this!

Operating in the data preparation space, Paxata allows business users to upload, investigate, and scrub incredibly large amounts of data in real time, giving them instant feedback as they shape, normalize, and filter to their hearts' content. While working here, I've collaborated with designers and teammates to undertake some colossal projects, from page redesigns to some of the largest features ever added to our product offering.

Adaptive Workload Management

In an effort to reduce system costs, Paxata embarked on a journey to let business analysts hop into a project and start working with their data before their imports finished. Users could setup their rules and logic on a subset of data and then operationalize their work to run against files exponentially larger than their working set, driving server costs lower and lower.

My part in this project was working on a complete frontend refactor of our Data Library portion of the product in order to simultaneously support our traditional and new workflows. Collaborating with our resident designer, I helped create a DSL wrapper around the popular table framework, ag-Grid, in order to provide a dynamic list experience composed of reusable table cells.

Intelligent Automation

For awhile now, Paxata has offered a single unit automation system. Want to bring in a MySQL table every Thursday? Done. How about crunching this project full of form data normalization? You got it. But most of the time, businesses don't need analytics in isolation; building solid business intelligence requires pulling, cleaning, morphing, and merging data from boundless sources. So why should you only be able to automate one piece at a time?

Enter Intelligent Automation, one of Paxata's most ambitious feature offerings to date. Based on how your data and projects interact, we will intelligently (see what I did there?) construct an entire graph of interwoven projects that you can configure and run as a single flow.

This happened to be the first major feature since I joined as the tech lead for our newly minted UI Team, which entailed mentoring and bringing our other frontend engineers up to speed on the current patterns and paradigms we use in our tech stack as well as architecting a totally new space in our application. We went through multiple phases of ideation, scoping, and tinkering to finally land with a product that could be launched in phased releases, progressively offering more and more functionality based on the target launch dates for some of our biggest customers.

IMS by Cardinal Health

Removing stress and hassle for patients and practitioners

IMS, or Inventory Management Solutions, is an all-in-one offering to aid in inventory management and drug administration for medical clinics all across the USA. By tracking patient appointments, anticipated prescriptions, and overall stock across entire fleets of pharmaceutical cabinets, IMS could manage a hospital's entire inventory and predict when new orders needed to be placed.

The software came in a two-fold package: a webapp that let hospital administrators or staff managers handle reporting, auditing, and ordering, and an embedded touchscreen application that lived on the cabinet machines which orchestrated drug dispersal, patient schedules, and inventory tracking in order to help practitioners treat their patients easily and quickly.

Battle Buddies

Tired of basic JS tutorials? Give this AI death arena a try

Built for Fuse's 2017 Code Camp, Battle Buddies is a multiplayer programming battle royale game for kids decently familiar with JavaScript who want to learn a smattering of HTTP requests, promises, and game theory. Every turn (about every second), a player's program would determine their best choice of action based on their bot's surrounding 2D environment, submit it to the server, and wait for the turn results before repeating the process over and over until only one bot was left standing.

The course proved to be a bit beefier than a day's worth of work, but by the end of the 13 hour day, everyone had semi-functional bots running around on the battlefield, trying for their chance for digital glory.

And I was tired. Very, very tired.

gleichsnerd

You're looking at it, and now I'm going to talk about it!

Most of this will seem like giberish unless you check out the retro styling portion of the website. Fair warning, there are a couple flashy elements at times and you will need to be on a browser (desktop is best) that supports WebGL and is JavaScript enabled.

I don't really understand how I got to this point, but I do remember waking up one Saturday morning thinking "what if I made a website that felt like before I even got hooked on the internet?" I fell back asleep for at least a few more hours, but I couldn't escape this idea of a wacky 2000s-era DVD experience to house my much-needed personal website and portfolio. I always wanted to dive into web graphics, and this was the perfect kick in the ass to get me going.

Of course, the retro version wasn't going to be everyone's glass of Tang and the eclectic nature of it would be inherently inaccessible, so I wanted to make a slimmed down modern version that focused on a clean, lightweight, and accessible experience that has just as much care put into it as my weird aesthetic experiment.
